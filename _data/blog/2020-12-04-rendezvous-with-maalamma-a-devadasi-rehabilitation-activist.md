The Devadasi System, prevalent in few southern Indian states, is a cruel system of marrying underage girls from marginalised communities to deities or Hindu priests of temples or objects of worship. This system is a form of modern day slavery and is popularly condemned to be “sacred prostitution”. The girl so married is forbidden from marrying any other mortal man and is perceived as duty bound to satisfy sexual urges of men of higher social status. This interview is the *IF Mag*azine’s experience of sneaking a peek into the mind of a grassroots activist, Ms. Maalamma who is actively fighting the Devdasi System.

Maalamma was born and brought up in Beylal, a village in Karnataka’s Bellary District. She is the General Secretary of Devadasi Mahila Vimochana Sangha and a member of the All India Democratic Women’s association, both organisations focus on development of women in rural areas. She says that her urge to fight the system day in and day out springs from the real life stories of thousands of women subject to the Devadasi System.

<br>

**How do women become subject to the Devadasi System?**

The system has its roots in the age old oppressive tradition of “muththu kattodhu” which translates as “tying of pearls”. The tradition entails the tying of pearls on the bodies of girls even before they attain puberty. Once the pearls are tied, it signifies that the girl is engaged to the God (in effect the priest) of a particular temple in the locality. Another tradition is the practice of throwing coloured water on girls during the community festivities of Ugadi (the local New Year) or Holi. This signifies that the girls are welcome to be devadasis.

When the *IF Mag*azine’’s team inquired Maalamma what would happen if these girls rejected being a Devadasi after such traditions have been imposed on them, she recalls an incident in 1984 when the huts of 3 Devdasi women were burnt because they refused to partake in the system anymore. She recalls that there were large scale protests against this incident under the leadership of one, Mr. Basavaraj.

Another incident she narrated was that of a head priest of a village who put forth in the Gram Panchayat that he had a vision that subjecting 2 minor girls to Devadasi system would bring welfare to the village. The Head priest’s suggestion was taken into account and the 2 minor girls were subject to the Devadasi system, says Maalamma. These real life incidents are what encouraged her to become a Devadasi rehabilitation activist, she remarks.

<br>

**Problems in Organising Women to fight against the system**

According to Maalamma, in 2011, the National Commission for Women estimated that there were 48,358 Devadasis in India. However, a 2015 Report by *Sampark* submitted to the International Labour Organisation (“Sampark Report”) estimates that the number of Devadasis all over India would be close to 4,50,000.

As an activist, Maalamma discusses the practical difficulties in organising women to fight against the system. She states that individual caste identities of the women play a massive role. Most women who are Devadasis are Dalit women. Merging Dalit women’s issues with non-dalit women’s issues to organise them together has multiple problems. Firstly, the issues faced by non-dalit women in rural areas are related to dowry and harassment. Their issues are massively different from the issues of the Dalit Devadasi women who fight for rights of their bastardised children, property etc. Secondly, the non-dalit women do not want to be seen as organised or fighting for Dalit Devadasi women as there is a social stigma attached to people associated with Devadasi women. Maalamma recalls instances where Non-Dalit women refused to sit with Dalit Devadasi women in gatherings organised by activists to bring about socio-legal awareness amongst rural women.

<br>

**Maalamma on what the law prohibiting Devadasi System does-**

The laws that prevent the Devadasi system in India are framed by State Legislatures and are thereby slightly different in application from one state to another. But the common factor among the Devadasi (Prohibition of Dedication) Acts is that the legislations are framed to penalise the dedication of girls and women as Devadasis instead of empowering them to not be a part of the system in the first place. The Act penalises anyone who abets or partakes in the dedication of women as a Devadasi to temples. The penalty is even higher when the abettor is a family member of the girl subject to the Devadasi system or the girl herself.

What the Act fails to realise is that Devadasis do not become subject to the system because they choose it but because their socio-economic status does not allow them an alternative or a choice. Most Devadasis’ mothers and grandmothers have also been Devadasis. The marginalisation of the segment of women who are generationally underprivileged is important and the legislations fail to realise it. Instead of enabling the women by giving them alternate job security, it prohibits and penalises the victims of generations of marginalisation.

Another aspect that the legislations fail to realise is that the system prevails not just because of the presence of Devadasis and abettors of Devadasi system but also due to the upper caste privileged men (priests and Feudalists). These men allow the system to prevail by consuming and benefiting from it. “The Act provides a very minor fine for these men who are in essence the culprits who endorse the system”, opines Maalamma.

<br>

**What are Devadasi Rehabilitation Activists fighting for?**

Maalamma States that the Devadasi Activists are fighting for the following issues:



a. A*bandonment of Children*

One of the system’s greatest results is the abandonment of children. The Devadasi System leads to a large number of children being abandoned not just by their biological fathers but also by their mothers. She recalls that in the initial years of her activism, she could not bear seeing the children not being enrolled in good schools owing to the fact that they were not allowed to enroll in schools without knowing their parents’ names. This is not just an issue of documentation alone, rather one that has a larger social stigma attached to it. Those children who finally make it to schools in the locality drop out due to bullying or negative treatment from their peers.\
\
b. *Property Rights*

Owing to bastardisation and abandonment of children, the Devadasis themselves and the children lack property rights. Despite being biological children of an upper caste man, they do not have access to the nourishment and property inheritance as that of children born out of socially recognised marriages.

*c. Pension and Job Security*

Since the Devadasi system is essentially a form of prostitution, the Devadasis are rendered jobless with old age but are denied obtaining of pensions/ retirement benefits. Though the National Commission of Women has come up with several schemes to provide pensions for these women, the beneficiaries are not as many owing to improper allocation of funds and collection of data regarding the actual number of devadasis in the particular area, says Maalamma.\
\
d. *Protection for migrants*

Another problem that the law fails to realise is that some women who cease to become a part of the system are eventually pushed out of their villages owing to social stigma. They are not offered new jobs owing to their past identities. Hence, they are forced to migrate to nearby towns/cities in search of new jobs, with a hope that their past identities will be erased. Such migration calls for rehabilitation facilities and protection from the Government.

*e. Right to Health and accessible Healthcare*

The Rehabilitation of Devadasis is impossible without ensuring that they are physically and emotionally healthy. Hence right to health and accessible healthcare is another everyday need of Devadasis that we, as a society, are ignoring.
